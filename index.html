A City's Ecological Collapse

Once known as the "Garden City," Bangalore has witnessed a staggering transformation. Over the last 50 years, rampant urbanization has decimated its green cover and water bodies, fundamentally altering the city's ability to sustain itself. This section visualizes the dramatic shift from a green oasis to a concrete landscape.

88% Loss of Vegetation

Green cover plummeted from 68.27% in 1973 to less than 15% today, crippling the city's lungs and biodiversity.

79% Loss of Water Bodies

The number of lakes fell from over 200 to just 93, with 98% of the remaining lakes encroached upon, severely impacting groundwater recharge.

1055% Growth in Pavement

Built-up area exploded from 8% to over 93% of the city's landscape, creating a water-scarce "concrete jungle."

The Path to Recovery

Restoring Bangalore's lakes is a complex, long-term commitment requiring a multi-faceted approach. From engineering solutions and ecological restoration to policy reform and community action, discover the key strategies that offer a blueprint for a healthier, water-secure future.

Aquatic Deweeding

Mechanical harvesters and manual labor are used to physically remove floating weeds like water hyacinth, which also removes the vast quantities of pollutants they absorb.

Manual Uprooting

Highly effective for terrestrial species like *Prosopis* and *Senna*. Removing the entire root system, especially after monsoons, prevents re-germination.

Cut Rootstock Method

A technique used for species like *Lantana* that minimizes soil disturbance while effectively controlling the plant.

Long-Term Commitment

Experts stress that removal is not a one-time event. It requires a 4-5 year continuous effort, immediately followed by native species restoration.

Desilting & Dredging

Fundamental to restoring a lake's original depth and water-holding capacity by removing decades of accumulated silt and pollutants.

Sewage Treatment Plants (STPs)

Essential infrastructure to stop the flow of untreated sewage, a primary driver of pollution and invasive weed growth.

Constructed Wetlands

Ecological engineering that uses native plants to naturally filter pollutants, absorb heavy metals, and improve water quality before it enters the lake.

Native Green Buffers

Planting native, non-invasive trees and plants around lake peripheries (not on lakebeds) to control erosion, filter runoff, and create healthy habitats.

Centralized Custodianship

Establishing a single, empowered agency to oversee all lakes, ensuring coordinated action and eliminating fragmented administration.

Strengthened Legal Frameworks

Enforcing laws like the KTCDA Act, which prohibits new plantations on lakebeds, and addressing loopholes for pre-existing encroachments.

Community Involvement

Empowering local citizen groups, NGOs, and ward committees to participate in monitoring, restoration, and decision-making, fostering a sense of ownership.

Prioritizing Native Species

Shifting policy from simply increasing "green cover" to strategically planting ecologically suitable native species in the right places.
